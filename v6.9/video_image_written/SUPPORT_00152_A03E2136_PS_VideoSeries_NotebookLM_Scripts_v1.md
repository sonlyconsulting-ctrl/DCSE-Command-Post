## EPISODE 02 | LEGAL FRAMEWORK
### "The Architecture of Proof: McDonnell Douglas, Darden, and the Rules of the Game"
**Duration Target:** 15–20 minutes
**Tab:** Legal Framework
**Background Use:** Study sessions, evening review

---

### DIALOGUE SCRIPT

**[HOST A]**
Episode Two. The legal framework. This is the foundation that everything else in this trial is built on. You cannot argue a fact without knowing which legal rule that fact is meant to prove. Today we're going to walk the full architecture.

**[HOST B]**
Let's start with the structure of a Title VII discrimination claim in a bench trial. The governing framework is McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green — Supreme Court, 1973. This is the burden-shifting framework that has governed employment discrimination litigation for over fifty years.

**[HOST A]**
Here's how it works. Step One — Plaintiff establishes a prima facie case. Four elements. First — Plaintiff is a member of a protected class. African American. That's admitted. Second — Plaintiff was qualified for the position. Thirty-plus years of IT experience. Zero contemporaneous performance concerns in the record. Third — Plaintiff suffered an adverse employment action. His assignment at DHHS ended on January 15, 2021. That's also not disputed. Fourth — similarly situated employees outside the protected class were treated more favorably. Trent Ballentine — white male, same team, same supervisors, retained. The prima facie case is established.

**[HOST B]**
Step Two — the burden shifts to DHHS. The defense has to articulate a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the termination. The threshold here is low. DHHS doesn't have to prove the reason is true. They just have to state one. And they will. They'll say — project completion, or performance.

**[HOST A]**
Step Three — the burden shifts back to Plaintiff. And this is where the case is won or lost. Plaintiff must show that DHHS's stated reason is a pretext — meaning it's not the real reason, or it's not the only reason, and discrimination was a motivating factor.

**[HOST B]**
And in a bench trial, pretext is proven by building what courts in this circuit have called a mosaic of circumstantial evidence. No single piece has to be a smoking gun. You build the picture tile by tile. Zero performance documentation. Ballentine retained. Overtime disparity in the data. Christensen's verbal "not working out" without a single written record. The direction of the removal going from DHHS to CSS, not CSS initiating it. Each piece adds to the mosaic.

**[HOST A]**
Now before we get to the merits of discrimination, we have to win the threshold question. Is DHHS even Plaintiff's employer under Title VII? Because Title VII only protects employees. Not independent contractors.

**[HOST B]**
And this is where the Darden test controls. Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. v. Darden, 503 U.S. 318 from 1992. The Supreme Court adopted the common law agency test for determining employee status under ERISA, and the Eighth Circuit applies that same framework to Title VII.

**[HOST A]**
The Eighth Circuit's application is explicit in Schwieger v. Farm Bureau Insurance, 207 F.3d 480, decided in 2000. The court lists the factors and says clearly — no single factor is controlling. You look at the totality of circumstances.

**[HOST B]**
Let's go through the factors that matter most for this case. Factor One — the hiring party's right to control the manner and means by which work is accomplished. Christensen assigned specific tasks. Converse supervised daily work product. The manner and means were controlled by DHHS, not CSS.

**[HOST A]**
Factor Two — the skill required. Senior IT consulting work in a specialized programming language. This cuts both ways — high skill can suggest independent contractor status. But in this case, the specific skill was the reason Plaintiff was placed on this specific DHHS team, integrated into their systems.

**[HOST B]**
Factor Three — the source of the instrumentalities and tools. DHHS provided the computer. DHHS provided email access, VPN credentials, access to NFOCUS and iServe. That's employer behavior, not client behavior.

**[HOST A]**
Factor Four — the duration of the relationship. Eight months. An extended, continuous relationship focused on a single client — DHHS. Not a short discrete project with multiple clients.

**[HOST B]**
Factor Five — whether the hiring party has the right to assign additional projects. Christensen assigned work through the Clarity system. That's an employer function.

**[HOST A]**
Factor Six — the extent of the hired party's discretion over when and how long to work. Converse approved timesheets. Hours were controlled. Overtime required approval. That is schedule control. That is an employer function.

**[HOST B]**
And here's the key point for trial. DHHS is going to say — CSS paid the wages, CSS issued the W-2, CSS handled all payroll obligations. Under Darden, that means CSS was the employer, not us.

**[HOST A]**
And the answer is — under Schwieger, that's one factor. The Eighth Circuit has explicitly said payroll mechanics alone do not determine employee status where other factors demonstrate the economic reality of employment. The word "alone" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

**[HOST B]**
Now let's map the six Title VII elements against the evidence so you can see them as a connected system, not a list.

**[HOST A]**
Element A — employee status. We just covered this. Darden factors run in Plaintiff's favor. DHHS controlled means, manner, schedule, tools, task assignment, and initiated the removal.

**[HOST B]**
Element B — protected class. African American male. Admitted.

**[HOST A]**
Element C — qualification. Plaintiff had thirty-plus years of IT experience. He met or exceeded performance expectations — proven by the absence of any written performance concern during his tenure. You cannot argue someone was unqualified while simultaneously producing zero documented performance issues.

**[HOST B]**
Element D — adverse employment action. Assignment terminated January 15, 2021. Admitted.

**[HOST A]**
Element E — disparate treatment. Ballentine, white male, retained on the same team, same supervisor chain. Traub receiving overtime at 37.8% while Plaintiff received zero. Both are in the Clarity data. The record speaks.

**[HOST B]**
Element F — pretext. Zero contemporaneous performance documentation. The only stated reason — Christensen's verbal "not working out" — has no written foundation. The removal decision came from DHHS. And Ballentine was retained. The pretext mosaic is built.

**[HOST A]**
Now one critical bench trial distinction. In a jury trial, you build a narrative. You tell a story that connects emotionally with twelve people. In a bench trial before Chief Judge Rossiter, you build a structure. You present numbered Proposed Findings of Fact tied directly to the evidentiary record.

**[HOST B]**
Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a), after a bench trial the court issues its own Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law. But Plaintiff submits Proposed Findings of Fact first — and those proposed findings frame the legal conclusions the court should adopt. This is DART Rule 11.4. Every argument must be formatted as a numbered finding, not a jury-oriented narrative.

**[HOST A]**
And in Chief Judge Rossiter's courtroom, that means logic, record citations, and legal authority. Not emotional appeals. Not dramatic narrative. Clean, precise, numbered findings tethered to the record.

**[HOST B]**
The legal architecture is in place. Episode Three maps the evidence — exhibit by exhibit, Bates number by Bates number, foundation witness by foundation witness.

**[HOST A]**
Second — these exports establish that the Clarity system was the mechanism through which DHHS exercised control over contractor schedules. Supervisors approved hours. Supervisors allocated time. **[HOST B]**
Now here's the critical gap. Converse's Clarity export — the supervisor records for Kim Converse — were demanded in December 2025. They have not been produced. DHHS produced native exports for the contractors but withheld the supervisor's records. The very records that would show how Converse exercised approval authority over Plaintiff's time compared to Ballentine's time.

**[HOST A]**
That asymmetric production is itself evidence. DHHS demonstrated that the Clarity system generates exportable supervisor records. They produced them for contractors. They withheld them for the supervisor who made the control decisions. That selective production prejudices the Darden analysis and warrants an adverse inference argument.

**[HOST B]**
Next exhibit — the email thread establishing the Chain of Decision. This is Exhibit A from the Rule 56(d) package, Doc 63-3. This thread establishes that DHHS communicated to Contract Staffing Specialists the direction to end Plaintiff's assignment. The decision traveled from DHHS outward. Not from CSS inward to DHHS.

**[HOST A]**
This is the exhibit that destroys the "mere contractor completion" narrative. In a contractor relationship, an assignment ends when the client decides it ends. And if the client is DHHS, and DHHS communicated the removal direction to CSS, then DHHS terminated the assignment. The contractor shield is not a shield when your own communications show you pulled the trigger.

**[HOST B]**
Foundation witness for that thread is Julie Christensen and CSS's Becky Fields. This is an impeachment exhibit. When Christensen or DHHS's counsel argues DHHS had no authority to terminate — this thread comes out.

**[HOST A]**
The performance documentation exhibit — which is, technically, an exhibit of absence. DHHS produced zero contemporaneous performance evaluations. Zero coaching logs. Zero PIPs. Zero written warnings. Across eight months of Plaintiff's tenure. Nothing.

**[HOST B]**
In federal employment discrimination law, the absence of documentation is itself evidence. You cannot claim a performance-based reason for termination that was never documented during the period of employment. That silence is the Ghost Performance doctrine. The foundation witness for this exhibit is Kim Converse and Julie Christensen — forced to admit under cross that no such documents exist.

**[HOST A]**
The Comparator Hour Analysis — Exhibit D, Doc 63-6. This exhibit presents the statistical analysis of hour allocation across contractors. The 37.8 percent versus zero percent differential. This exhibit is trial-ready. Foundation witness is Plaintiff's declaration and potentially an expert on statistical employment analysis.

**[HOST B]**
Christensen's "not working out" statement. This lives in discovery responses and the complaint record. It is the only stated performance concern in the record. No document created contemporaneously corroborates it. This is both an impeachment tool — she said it without a single supporting document — and a pretext exhibit. A reason that was never written down before the termination decision looks exactly like a reason that was invented after the decision was made.

**[HOST A]**
The Nebraska Contractor Employment Waiver — Doc 60-5, Seals-TitleVII-000148. This is DHHS's contractor shield document. Plaintiff signed an acknowledgment that he was a CSS employee, not a State of Nebraska employee.

**[HOST B]**
Here's the rebuttal. The waiver addresses benefits eligibility — it says if Plaintiff is found to be a State employee, he voluntarily waives state benefits in favor of CSS benefits. It is not a legal adjudication of employment status. The Darden test is a multi-factor functional analysis. A signed benefits waiver does not override the common law agency test. Courts have held this repeatedly.

**[HOST A]**
The Privilege Log Deficiency — Exhibit F, Doc 63-8. DHHS withheld documents and logged them as privileged. Among the withheld items — meeting notes by Courtney Jerke from a conversation between a DHHS attorney and Plaintiff's supervisors about preparing their response to Plaintiff's EEOC claim. Withheld under attorney-client privilege.

**[HOST B]**
The challenge to that log is that the Rule 26(b)(5) requirements were not met. A privilege log must describe the document in a way that allows the opposing party to assess the claim. Meeting notes involving HR personnel and supervisors — not just attorneys — raise questions about whether the communication was made for the purpose of obtaining legal advice, or for operational purposes. If it was operational, the privilege claim fails.

**[HOST A]**
The ASPX files — Exhibit E, Doc 63-7. Two documents were produced only in .aspx format — essentially, web server navigation URLs that cannot be reviewed without access to DHHS's internal SharePoint system. Those documents are not reviewable in their produced form. DHHS agreed to re-produce them in a reviewable format. That re-production has not been confirmed as complete.

**[HOST B]**
Now let's cover the Motions in Limine targets. Due August 17, 2026. Under DART Rule 11.3, we're targeting three categories for exclusion.

**[HOST A]**
First — any post-hoc performance justification created after January 15, 2021. Under Federal Rules of Evidence 401 and 403, such a document has no probative value as to the contemporaneous reason for termination, and its admission would be severely prejudicial. A document written after the decision was made cannot retroactively explain the reason for the decision.

**[HOST B]**
Second — any character evidence or prior conduct not produced in discovery that DHHS attempts to introduce at trial. Federal Rule of Evidence 404 bars character evidence offered to prove action in conformity therewith. If DHHS didn't produce it in discovery, they don't get to use it at trial.

**[HOST A]**
Third — hearsay in CSS communications that DHHS attempts to introduce without proper business record foundation. If DHHS wants to use CSS communications for the truth of the matter asserted, they need to lay a business record foundation under Federal Rule of Evidence 803(6). That foundation requires a custodian or other qualified witness with knowledge of how those records were kept.

**[HOST B]**
The stipulated facts that Plaintiff should seek to lock in before the pretrial conference. Plaintiff is African American — admitted. Plaintiff's assignment ended January 15, 2021 — admitted. Trent Ballentine is a white male who began his DHHS assignment on the same date as Plaintiff — admitted. Ballentine and Plaintiff shared the same supervisory chain — Converse, Christensen, Schafers — admitted. CSS issued Plaintiff's wages and W-2 — admitted, but not dispositive on Darden.

**[HOST A]**
Every admitted fact is a fact Justin J. Hall cannot dispute at trial. Lock those admissions in the Pretrial Order. They cannot be relitigated.

**[HOST B]**
Episode Four — witness preparation. We go witness by witness, question by question, with the impeachment trap for each.

## EPISODE 04 | WITNESS PREPARATION
### "In the Box: Cross-Examining Converse, Christensen, and Schafers"
**Duration Target:** 18–25 minutes
**Tab:** Witness Prep
**Background Use:** Pre-trial rehearsal, morning of trial prep

---

### DIALOGUE SCRIPT

**[HOST A]**
Episode Four. Witness Preparation. This is the most tactical episode in the series. We are going witness by witness through the hostile examination strategy for Converse, Christensen, and Schafers. By the end of this episode, you should be able to hear any of these witnesses' voices in your head and know exactly what question comes next.

**[HOST B]**
Before we go witness by witness — five rules for cross-examining hostile witnesses in a federal bench trial. These are non-negotiable.

**[HOST A]**
Rule One — only closed, leading questions. A leading question contains its own answer. "You approved Mr. Seals' timesheets in Clarity, correct?" That is a leading question. "What did you do with Mr. Seals' timesheets?" That is not. Never ask a hostile witness an open question. You do not know where they will go.

**[HOST B]**
Rule Two — have the contradicting document in hand before you ask the impeachment question. If you're going to confront Converse with an email showing DHHS directed CSS to remove Plaintiff, you hold that document up before you ask the question. Not after. The document comes out when the witness denies the fact you're about to prove.

**[HOST A]**
Rule Three — pause after a damaging admission. Let it land. Federal judges take notes. When a witness admits something damaging, the judge is writing it down. If you immediately move to the next question, you dilute the moment. Let the admission sit in the air.

**[HOST B]**
Rule Four — address the judge when you need to make a legal point from an answer. If a witness gives an evasive answer that opens a legal issue, you turn to Chief Judge Rossiter and address the court directly. "Your Honor, the witness has not answered the question. The question was..." You are trying the case to the judge, not to the witness.

**[HOST A]**
Rule Five — object specifically. Not generally. Not "I object." Specifically — "Hearsay, Your Honor." "Foundation, Your Honor." "Calls for speculation, Your Honor." State the rule. Do not argue. The judge knows the rules. Your job is to invoke them precisely.

**[HOST B]**
Now. Kim Converse.

**[HOST A]**
Converse is the direct supervisor. She is the most important Darden witness. Her Clarity timesheet approvals are the spine of the employer-control analysis. Her examination theme is Control.

**[HOST B]**
Question One to Converse. "Ms. Converse, you were Donald Seals' direct supervisor at DHHS during his assignment from August 2020 through January 2021, correct?" You are locking in the supervisory relationship. She cannot deny it. The record is clear.

**[HOST A]**
Question Two. "And you approved Mr. Seals' time entries in the Clarity system during that period, correct?" You are nailing Darden Factor Three — schedule control. If she says yes, you have the admission. If she says she merely reviewed them, you have your follow-up — "But they could not be processed for payment without your approval, correct?" That's still approval authority.

**[HOST B]**
Question Three. "You also approved Trent Ballentine's time entries in the Clarity system during the same period, correct?" Same supervisor. Same system. Same approval authority over both contractors. This establishes that the identical mechanism of control was exercised over the comparator. One hand on both levers.

**[HOST A]**
Question Four. "Ms. Converse, during Mr. Seals' eight-month tenure at DHHS, you never issued him a written performance warning, correct?" She has to say no. Nothing was produced. "And you never issued a performance improvement plan — a PIP — to Mr. Seals, correct?" No. "And there are no coaching logs or written performance evaluations for Mr. Seals in the DHHS record, correct?" No.

**[HOST B]**
Three consecutive no answers. No documentation. No documentation. No documentation. That is the Ghost Performance doctrine built live in the courtroom, from the witness's own mouth.

**[HOST A]**
Question Five. "You communicated with Contract Staffing Specialists about ending Mr. Seals' assignment, correct?" This is the Chain of Decision question. If she says she had no communication — you bring out Exhibit A, the email thread. "I'm showing you what has been marked as Plaintiff's Exhibit A. Do you recognize this email thread?" Authentication. Then — "And this email reflects a communication between DHHS personnel and CSS regarding Mr. Seals' assignment, correct?" Established.

**[HOST B]**
Julie Christensen.

**[HOST A]**
Christensen is the manager. She made the "not working out" statement. She is your pretext witness. Her examination theme is Documentation — or the complete absence of it.

**[HOST B]**
Question One. "Ms. Christensen, you served as Mr. Seals' manager during his assignment at DHHS, correct?" Established.

**[HOST A]**
Question Two. "At some point, you made the statement that Mr. Seals was 'not working out,' correct?" Lock in the statement. She cannot deny it. It's in the record. Now the statement is on the table, attributed, and attributed without any written foundation.

**[HOST B]**
Question Three. "Prior to making that statement, you had never issued Mr. Seals a written performance warning, correct?" No. "You had never issued him a performance improvement plan, correct?" No. "And there are no written coaching logs from you to Mr. Seals during his tenure, correct?" No.

**[HOST A]**
Then — "So on the date you said Mr. Seals was 'not working out,' there was not a single piece of written documentation in the DHHS record that had been created during his tenure to support that characterization. Is that correct?"

**[HOST B]**
That question has to be answered. And the only honest answer is yes. That's correct. Which means the stated performance reason had no documentary foundation created while Plaintiff was employed. Which means it was invented after the decision to remove him. That is pretext, built from Christensen's own testimony.

**[HOST A]**
Question Four. "During this same period, Trent Ballentine continued his assignment without receiving any documented performance concerns from you, correct?" Established. Same manager. One contractor terminated. One retained. No documentation for either. Race is the differential.

**[HOST B]**
Question Five. "You assigned Mr. Seals specific tasks related to the NFOCUS and iServe systems, correct?" Darden Factor Five — work integral to DHHS's core operations. These aren't peripheral tasks. These are the state government's human services information systems.

**[HOST A]**
Lisa Schafers.

**[HOST B]**
Schafers is the executive manager. Her examination is shorter but establishes the institutional level of the discriminatory decision. Her theme is Authority — executive awareness.

**[HOST A]**
Question One. "Ms. Schafers, you were the executive-level manager with oversight responsibility for the team that included both Donald Seals and Trent Ballentine during 2020 and 2021, correct?" Established.

**[HOST B]**
Question Two. "You had visibility into resourcing decisions for that team — including decisions about which contractors to retain or release from their assignments — correct?" This establishes executive-level involvement in the decision to remove Plaintiff while retaining Ballentine.

**[HOST A]**
Question Three. "You are not aware of any written performance documentation for Mr. Seals that was created before the decision was made to end his assignment, correct?" Executive-level confirmation of the Ghost Performance doctrine. The absence of documentation rises all the way to the executive chain.

**[HOST B]**
If Schafers says she was not involved in the day-to-day performance management — that's acceptable. ## EPISODE 05 | DAMAGES
### "The Math and the Merit: Building the Damages Case"
**Duration Target:** 12–15 minutes
**Tab:** Damages
**Background Use:** Evening session, quiet review

---

### DIALOGUE SCRIPT

**[HOST A]**
Episode Five. Damages. This episode covers both the numbers and the legal framework for presenting the damages case to Chief Judge Rossiter. In a bench trial, the judge is the fact-finder for damages. There is no jury sympathy. There is record, calculation, and law.

**[HOST B]**
Let's start with back pay. Back pay is an equitable remedy under Title VII. It is not subject to the statutory damages cap. The calculation basis is the invoiced hourly rate of $72 per hour, times the number of work hours per year.

**[HOST A]**
The annual hours are calculated from 303 working days — that's a standard work year excluding weekends and federal holidays — times 8 hours per day. That gives you 2,424 hours per year. At $72 per hour, that's $174,528 per year in gross back pay, before tax considerations.

**[HOST B]**
The back pay period runs from January 15, 2021 — the date Plaintiff's assignment was terminated — through the date of judgment. The longer the case runs, the larger the back pay figure grows. At the original five-year horizon projected in the complaint, the gross calculation reaches $872,640.

**[HOST A]**
Important note — the court will also consider whether back pay should be reduced by any interim earnings. If Plaintiff obtained other employment during the back pay period, those earnings offset the award. Document all employment and income from January 2021 forward.

**[HOST B]**
Front pay. Also equitable — not subject to the cap. Front pay covers the period from judgment forward, through the point at which Plaintiff could reasonably be expected to obtain comparable employment — or through the end of working life, if reinstatement is impracticable.

**[HOST A]**
In this case, reinstatement to the DHHS contractor team is almost certainly impracticable. The working relationship has been too adversarial. The court will be asked to calculate a front pay award based on reasonable projected future earning capacity.

**[HOST B]**
Compensatory damages — emotional distress. This requires personal testimony. Plaintiff must describe the nature of the emotional harm, its duration, its impact on daily life, relationships, health, and professional standing. Supporting documentation — therapy records, medical records, personal journals — strengthens the award. This is a credibility-based determination. The judge will assess the testimony directly.

**[HOST A]**
Punitive damages. Title VII allows punitive damages where the employer acted with malice or with reckless indifference to the plaintiff's federally protected rights. But two critical limits apply.

**[HOST B]**
First — the statutory cap. Under Title VII, combined compensatory and punitive damages are capped at $300,000 for employers with 500 or more employees. DHHS as a state agency almost certainly exceeds that threshold. So the cap applies to the combined compensatory-plus-punitive award. Back pay and front pay are not subject to this cap.

**[HOST A]**
Second — punitive damages against a state agency under Title VII are contested. The Supreme Court's sovereign immunity jurisprudence creates complexity here. DHHS will likely argue it is immune from punitive damages as a state entity. This issue must be briefed and addressed in the Proposed Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law.

**[HOST B]**
The documented total damages figure in the DCSE PS case profile is approximately $783,819.84. That figure represents the combined estimate across back pay, front pay, compensatory, and punitive components, calibrated against the applicable statutory caps.

**[HOST A]**
Now the defense's expected attack on damages — the mitigation duty. Under Title VII, a plaintiff has an affirmative duty to mitigate damages by making reasonable efforts to find comparable employment. If Plaintiff fails to mitigate — by not applying for comparable IT consulting positions — the court can reduce the back pay award.

**[HOST B]**
Critical point — mitigation is an affirmative defense. The burden is on DHHS to prove that Plaintiff failed to mitigate. It is not on Plaintiff to prove that mitigation was impossible. Hall will need to produce evidence that comparable positions were available and Plaintiff failed to pursue them. If that evidence doesn't exist, the mitigation argument fails.

**[HOST A]**
Plaintiff's obligation is to document the job search. Every application, every response, every offer or rejection. That record becomes the rebuttal to any mitigation argument Hall raises.

**[HOST B]**
Open item — OI-3 from the TRIBUNAL_SYNC document. The damages reconciliation worksheet is pending authorization. That worksheet must be completed before the Pretrial Order Section J is finalized. August 19 deadline. This is a blocking item.

## EPISODE 06 | LEGAL ROUNDTABLE
### "War Game: Plaintiff v. Defense in the Courtroom Simulation"
**Duration Target:** 20–28 minutes
**Tab:** Roundtable
**Background Use:** Deep strategy sessions, the night before any major filing

---

### DIALOGUE SCRIPT

**[HOST A]**
Episode Six. The Legal Roundtable. This is the adversarial simulation episode. We're going to run the key contested issues of this trial as a structured debate — Plaintiff's position against the modeled defense — and then apply what we call the Bench Perspective. How is Chief Judge Rossiter likely to weigh this argument?

**[HOST B]**
Four voices today. Plaintiff's position, which is our home base. Defense position, modeled from Justin J. Hall's anticipated strategy based on the briefing record in Docs 60 through 68. Senior Legal Analyst, providing the structural assessment. And Bench Perspective, applying what we know about how federal bench trial judges evaluate these categories of evidence.

**[HOST A]**
Issue One. Employment Status. Is DHHS Donald Seals' employer under Title VII?

**[HOST B — DEFENSE MODEL]**
The record is unambiguous. Contract Staffing Specialists, Inc. was Mr. Seals' employer. CSS paid his wages. CSS issued his Form W-2. CSS administered all employment-related obligations. DHHS's role was limited to ordinary project oversight — reviewing deliverables and approving hours for budget tracking purposes. That is not employer control. That is contract administration. Under Darden, the entity that pays wages and issues tax documentation is a strong indicator of employer status. The entirety of CSS's operational relationship with Mr. Seals places employer status squarely with CSS, not DHHS.

**[HOST A — PLAINTIFF]**
The Eighth Circuit's instruction in Schwieger v. Farm Bureau is clear — no single factor is controlling. CSS's payroll role is one factor in a multi-factor analysis. When you run the other factors: Kim Converse approved every timesheet. Julie Christensen assigned every task. DHHS provided the computer, the credentials, the VPN. Plaintiff was integrated into NFOCUS and iServe — the state's core operational systems. And critically — the removal decision originated from DHHS and was communicated outward to CSS. Under Darden, the holistic picture shows DHHS as the functional employer.

**[ANALYST]**
The legal standard here is clear: holistic analysis, no single factor dispositive. Defense's strongest point is the payroll mechanics plus the contractor waiver signed by Plaintiff. Plaintiff's strongest counter is the Clarity timesheet approval authority and the direction of the removal communication. The key trial question is whether the task-assignment evidence and the removal communication are sufficient to overcome the payroll mechanics evidence. In the Eighth Circuit, employer-control of daily task assignment has been treated as a high-weight Darden factor.

**[BENCH PERSPECTIVE]**
Chief Judge Rossiter will expect this issue to be briefed in the Proposed Findings of Fact with numbered findings mapped to each Darden factor. The Court will want to see each factor assessed against specific record evidence — not general arguments. Plaintiff should present a Darden factor-by-factor analysis as numbered findings, with record citations for each. The absence of Converse's supervisor Clarity export is a gap — Plaintiff should request an adverse inference instruction or note the selective production in the findings.

**[HOST B]**
Issue Two. The Comparator. Is Trent Ballentine a valid comparator for the similarly-situated analysis?

**[HOST A — DEFENSE MODEL]**
The Eighth Circuit requires that comparators be similarly situated in all material respects. Mr. Ballentine and Mr. Seals did not perform identical work. Their specific technical assignments, project deliverables, and performance histories differed. The mere fact that they shared a supervisory chain is insufficient to establish comparator validity. Plaintiff must show that Ballentine's circumstances were materially identical in all relevant dimensions — not merely that they worked in the same general environment.

**[HOST B — PLAINTIFF]**
The Court itself addressed this at the initial review stage — in the order appearing at Doc 60-2 — and found that the allegations as to Ballentine met the pleading standard for comparator validity. Ballentine and Plaintiff began on the same date. Same supervisor: Converse. Same manager: Christensen. Same executive: Schafers. Same programming language — a specific and unique one. Similar experience levels. The supervisory chain is not merely a general environment — it is the specific decision-making structure that controlled both contractors. When the same people making decisions about one contractor make different decisions about another contractor of a different race, that comparison is valid.

**[ANALYST]**
The Eighth Circuit's "similarly situated in all material respects" standard requires comparators to have dealt with the same supervisor, engaged in the same conduct, and been subject to the same standards. Ballentine satisfies all three prongs. The defense's "different specific assignments" argument is a red herring — both contractors were on the same team, the same supervisory chain, assessed by the same people. The Court's own initial review already found the comparator allegations sufficient. That finding doesn't bind the trial, but it is persuasive context.

**[BENCH PERSPECTIVE]**
Rossiter will look at the specific decision-makers. If the same individuals who made the decision about Seals made the decision about Ballentine — and the outcomes diverged along racial lines — that is the comparator. The overtime disparity in the Clarity data is particularly powerful because it is numerical and contemporaneous. Rossiter does not need the parties to interpret it. The data speaks. Present Exhibit D early in the trial.

**[HOST A]**
Issue Three. Pretext. Did DHHS terminate Plaintiff for a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason, or was the stated reason pretextual?

**[HOST B — DEFENSE MODEL]**
DHHS terminated Mr. Seals' assignment due to project completion and performance concerns. These are legitimate, non-discriminatory business reasons. The absence of written documentation does not transform a legitimate business decision into pretext. Not every employment decision requires a paper trail. Mr. Seals' supervisors observed performance issues in real time and made a reasonable business judgment to end the assignment.

**[HOST A — PLAINTIFF]**
Performance concerns that were never written down during an eight-month assignment, never communicated to Plaintiff in writing, never formalized in any PIP or coaching document, and never produced in discovery — those are not "real-time observations." They are post-hoc rationalizations. The McDonnell Douglas framework exists precisely to smoke out these situations. A stated reason with zero documentary foundation, generated after the termination decision, is the textbook definition of pretext. Additionally — Ballentine was retained. If the stated reason were genuine, and both contractors were on the same team with the same supervisor, why did only the Black contractor get terminated while the white contractor continued?

**[ANALYST]**
Pretext by absence of documentation is a well-established theory in the Eighth Circuit. The Ghost Performance doctrine — zero contemporaneous records across eight months — is a strong pretext argument. Combined with Ballentine's retention, the overtime disparity, and the direction of the removal communication, the pretext mosaic is substantial. Defense needs to produce some contemporaneous evidence of performance concern. They haven't. That's the problem.

**[BENCH PERSPECTIVE]**
Rossiter is a former federal prosecutor. He evaluates credibility with precision. When a witness claims there were performance problems but cannot produce a single document created during the performance period to substantiate that claim, Rossiter will assess that testimony carefully. The cross-examination sequences for Converse and Christensen must be disciplined and efficient — extract the admissions, let the record speak, and move on. Do not let hostile witnesses fill the air with narrative. Control the examination.

**[HOST B]**
Issue Four. The Bench Trial itself. What does the format of the trial mean for Plaintiff's presentation strategy?

**[HOST A]**
No jury. Everything is to Rossiter. He is not moved by emotional narrative. He is moved by structured, record-tethered legal analysis presented in the format he expects — which is numbered Proposed Findings of Fact mapped to specific evidence. Every argument you make in opening, every exhibit you introduce, every admission you extract — all of it feeds into the Proposed Findings document you will submit before or during trial.

**[HOST B]**
Think of it this way. In a jury trial, you're telling a story. In a bench trial, you're building a legal architecture. Chief Judge Rossiter will write his own Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law after the trial. Your job is to give him the materials to build with. Every fact in the record, every admission from a hostile witness, every exhibit authenticated — those are the bricks. Your Proposed Findings are the blueprint. Make the blueprint so clean and record-grounded that Rossiter can adopt large portions of it directly into his written order.

**[HOST A]**
